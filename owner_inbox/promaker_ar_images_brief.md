## Research Findings

### What AR Assembly Interfaces Look Like

Modern AR assembly apps — including Scope AR WorkLink, PTC Vuforia, IKEA's AssembleAR, and various industrial guidance tools — share a consistent visual language. The phone camera feed occupies the full screen as a live backdrop. Overlaid on top sits a semi-transparent 3D model rendered true-to-scale and anchored to a physical surface or object. Colored highlights (typically bright cyan, orange, or green) draw attention to the component currently being acted on. Directional arrows — linear for sliding, curved for rotation — animate to indicate motion. Thin spatially-anchored callout lines connect labels to specific parts. Step counters appear in a floating HUD (heads-up display) panel at the top or bottom of the screen, usually minimal: "Step 3 of 12." The overall UI stays sparse — the AR content IS the instruction; text is kept to a minimum to avoid cluttering the camera view.

### Visual References and Design Patterns from Research

The most compelling demo images from funded AR startups (WayRay, Scope AR, JigSpace) share several traits: (1) a clean modern phone mockup — typically an iPhone or high-end Android — held at a natural angle, not overhead; (2) a photorealistic environment in the background (living room, workshop, garage floor) rather than a plain white backdrop; (3) the AR overlay rendered with ambient-occlusion shading so it looks physically grounded, not "pasted on"; (4) one or two bright accent colors on top of a near-neutral base. IKEA's AssembleAR demo images show the paper manual lying on the floor alongside the phone — a powerful before/after visual that makes the product concept instantly clear even without reading a caption. Pitch decks that perform well with investors use a full-bleed hero image on slide 1 — no text except a logo — then introduce the problem/solution visual pair on slides 2 and 3.

### Design Direction Informed by Research

For BuildARPro the strongest visual approach is: photorealistic + cinematic, not cartoon/illustration. The product competes in a serious B2B-adjacent space (industrial/consumer assembly) and needs to feel credible and polished. Accent colors should be electric blue (#00A3FF) and neon green (#39FF14) against dark, desaturated environments — a combination that reads as "tech precision" while the warm human elements (hands, furniture, wooden floors) keep it approachable. Clean sans-serif typography (inter or SF Pro) for any on-screen UI text in mock-ups.

## Design Direction for Pitch Deck

**Color palette:**
- Primary background: Deep navy `#0D1B2A`
- Primary accent: Electric blue `#00A3FF`
- Secondary accent: Neon green `#39FF14` (use sparingly — CTA moments only)
- Text: White `#FFFFFF` on dark, dark charcoal `#1C1C1E` on light slides
- Neutral: Warm off-white `#F5F0EB` for lifestyle/aspirational slides

**Typography:**
- Headlines: Inter or SF Pro Display, Bold, 48–60pt
- Body: Inter Regular, 18–20pt
- UI callouts in mockups: SF Pro Text, 14pt

**Visual tone:**
Cinematic and precise. Not a gaming app, not a gimmick — this should feel like an Apple product launch presentation. Every slide should have one dominant visual element and minimal text. The AR overlay in images should always look grounded (ambient occlusion, proper perspective) — never "floaty" or obviously composited. Use the paper manual as a consistent visual anchor: it represents the "before" in every slide and the phone+AR represents the "after."

**Slide structure recommendation:**
1. Hero (Image 1) — full bleed, logo only
2. Problem (Image 2) — one headline, one supporting stat
3. Solution (Image 3) — one-sentence value prop + app mockup
4. How It Works (Image 6) — 3-step pipeline diagram
5. Feature Detail (Image 4) — close-up of guidance UI
6. Outcome (Image 5) — aspirational lifestyle + key metric
